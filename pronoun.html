Pronouns Grammar Guide

A complete reference for understanding different types of pronouns in English grammar. Each pronoun type is covered in detail with examples and usage rules.

Personal Pronouns

Personal pronouns replace specific nouns referring to people or things. They change form based on their grammatical role in a sentence.

Examples:

Subject Pronouns: I, you, he, she, it, we, they
Object Pronouns: me, you, him, her, it, us, them
Usage Note: Subject pronouns are used when the pronoun is the subject of the verb. Object pronouns are used when the pronoun is the object of a verb or preposition.

Possessive Pronouns

Possessive pronouns show ownership or possession without the need for an apostrophe.

Examples:

Possessive Pronouns: mine, yours, his, hers, its, ours, theirs
Possessive Adjectives: my, your, his, her, its, our, their
Usage Note: Possessive pronouns stand alone ("This book is mine"), while possessive adjectives modify nouns ("This is my book").

Demonstrative Pronouns

Demonstrative pronouns point to specific things, indicating their distance from the speaker.

Examples:

Singular: this (near), that (far)
Plural: these (near), those (far)
Usage Note: Demonstrative pronouns can also function as demonstrative adjectives when they modify a noun ("This book is interesting" vs. "This is interesting").

Interrogative Pronouns

Interrogative pronouns are used to ask questions.

Examples:

For people: who, whom, whose
For things: what, which
Usage Note: Use who for the subject of a question, whom for the object (though increasingly less common in informal English), and whose to ask about possession.

Relative Pronouns

Relative pronouns introduce relative clauses, connecting them to the noun they modify.

Examples:

For people: who, whom, whose
For things: which, that
Usage Note: That can often be used for both people and things in restrictive clauses (clauses essential to the sentence's meaning), while who and which are preferred in non-restrictive clauses (additional information).

Indefinite Pronouns

Indefinite pronouns refer to non-specific people or things.

Examples:

Singular: someone, anyone, nobody, everybody, something, nothing
Plural: both, few, many, several
Singular or Plural: all, any, more, most, none, some
Usage Note: Most indefinite pronouns are singular and take singular verbs ("Everyone is here"). The pronouns that can be singular or plural depend on what they refer to ("All of the cake is gone" vs. "All of the students are here").
