Personal Pronouns

Refer to specific people or things. Change form based on person (1st, 2nd, 3rd), number (singular/plural), and case (subject/object).

Subject vs. Object:

Subject: I, you, he, she, it, we, they
Object: me, you, him, her, it, us, them
She called me. / They saw us.

Use subject pronouns before verbs (who performs action). Object pronouns after verbs or prepositions (who receives action).

Possessive Forms

Show ownership or possession. Two forms: possessive adjectives (before nouns) and possessive pronouns (stand alone).

Examples:

Adjective: my, your, his, her, its, our, their
Pronoun: mine, yours, his, hers, its, ours, theirs
My book → The book is mine.

⚠️ No apostrophes in possessive pronouns: yours, hers, ours, theirs. Its (possession) vs. it's (it is).

Demonstrative Pronouns

Point to specific things based on distance (near/far) and number (singular/plural).

This, That, These, Those:

This (near/singular): This is delicious.
That (far/singular): That looks amazing.
These (near/plural): These are fresh.
Those (far/plural): Those belong to Tom.

Can act as pronouns (This is mine) or determiners (This book). Distance can be physical or temporal.

Reflexive & Intensive

Both use -self/selves forms. Reflexive reflects action back to subject; intensive emphasizes the subject.

Examples:

Reflexive: I cut myself. (necessary)
Intensive: I myself saw it. (emphasis)
Forms: myself, yourself, himself, herself, itself, ourselves, yourselves, themselves

💡 Reflexive pronouns are essential to meaning; intensive can be removed without changing the core meaning.

Relative Pronouns

Introduce relative clauses and connect them to a noun/pronoun. Choose based on person/thing and subject/object function.

Who, Whom, Which, That:

Who: people (subject) — The man who called
Whom: people (object) — The woman whom I met
Which: animals/things — The book which I read
That: people/animals/things — The car that I bought

In defining clauses, "that" is common. Use "whom" after prepositions or as object (formal).

Case & Agreement Rules

Pronouns must agree with their antecedents in number, gender, and person. Use correct case (subjective, objective, possessive).

Key Rules:

Compound subjects: John and I (not me)
After prepositions: between you and me (not I)
Singular they: "Everyone brought their lunch."

Avoid common errors: Me and her went → She and I went. Subject pronouns for subjects, object pronouns for objects.
