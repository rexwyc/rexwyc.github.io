Phrasal Verbs

Multi-word verbs combining a base verb with particles (prepositions/adverbs). Meaning often differs from the original verb — essential for fluent, natural English.

Key examples:

give up → quit
run into → meet unexpectedly
look forward to → anticipate with pleasure

Phrasal verbs can be separable or inseparable. Some have multiple meanings based on context. Master them to sound like a native speaker.

Finite vs Non-Finite Verbs

Finite verbs show tense, person, and number; they anchor independent clauses. Non-finite verbs (infinitives, gerunds, participles) don’t change with the subject.

Illustrations:

Finite: She walks to school. (tense: present, 3rd person)
Non-Finite: She wants to walk home. (infinitive)
Walking is fun. (gerund)

Every full sentence needs at least one finite verb. Non-finite verbs often appear in subordinate structures.

Action vs Stative Verbs

Action verbs describe dynamic activities (run, build, speak). Stative verbs describe states, emotions, possession, or senses (know, believe, own). Stative verbs rarely appear in continuous tenses.

Quick comparison:

Action: I am running a marathon.
Stative: I love chocolate. (NOT I am loving)
Borderline: taste / think — can be both.

Knowing the difference prevents common tense mistakes and adds nuance to your writing.

Main vs Helping Verbs

Main verbs carry the core meaning. Helping verbs (auxiliaries: be, do, have, modals) accompany main verbs to form tenses, questions, negatives, and voice.

Structure examples:

Main verb only: She writes novels.
Helping + Main: She has been writing for years.
Modal helper: You must finish.

Modal auxiliaries (can, might, should) add mood. Identify them to perfect complex sentences.

Transitive vs Intransitive Verbs

Transitive verbs require a direct object to complete meaning. Intransitive verbs do not take an object. Some verbs can function both ways.

At a glance:

Transitive: She carries a bag. (object: a bag)
Intransitive: He arrived late. (no object)
Both: She reads a book / She reads every day.
