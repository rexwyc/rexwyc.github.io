Main vs. Helping Verbs

Main verbs express the primary action or state of being. Helping verbs (auxiliary verbs) help the main verb express tense, mood, or voice.

Examples:

Main: She reads every day.
Helping: She is reading now.
Both: They have been studying.

Common helping verbs: be, have, do, will, would, can, could, may, might, shall, should, must.

Action vs. Stative Verbs

Action verbs express physical or mental activities. Stative verbs describe states, conditions, feelings, or possession rather than actions.

Examples:

Action: run, jump, think, build
Stative: know, believe, own, prefer
Context: I think (action) vs. I think (stative)

Stative verbs are typically not used in continuous tenses (-ing forms).

Transitive vs. Intransitive

Transitive verbs require a direct object to complete their meaning. Intransitive verbs do not require a direct object.

Examples:

Transitive: She bought a car.
Intransitive: He arrived early.
Both: She runs (intr.) / She runs a business (trans.)

Some verbs can be both transitive and intransitive depending on usage.

Phrasal Verbs

Phrasal verbs consist of a verb plus one or more particles (prepositions or adverbs) that create a new meaning different from the original verb.

Examples:

Literal: look up (direct your gaze upward)
Phrasal: look up (search for information)
Common: give up, break down, turn down

Phrasal verbs can be separable or inseparable, and transitive or intransitive.

Finite vs. Non-Finite

Finite verbs show tense and agree with subjects. Non-finite verbs (infinitives, gerunds, participles) do not show tense or subject agreement.

Examples:

Finite: She writes letters.
Non-Finite: She likes to write letters.
Forms: to write (infin.), writing (gerund), written (participle)

Every sentence must contain at least one finite verb.

Regular vs. Irregular

Regular verbs form their past tense and past participle by adding -ed. Irregular verbs have unique forms for past tense and past participle.

Examples:

Regular: walk → walked → walked
Irregular: go → went → gone
Irregular: be → was/were → been

There are about 200 irregular verbs in English that must be memorized individually.

Main Verbs vs. Helping Verbs

Understanding the difference between main verbs and helping verbs (also called auxiliary verbs) is fundamental to English grammar. Main verbs carry the core meaning of the sentence, while helping verbs assist in expressing grammatical concepts.

Key Characteristics:

  • Main Verbs: Express the primary action or state of being
  • Helping Verbs: Help the main verb express tense, mood, voice, or aspect
  • Position: Helping verbs come before main verbs
  • Multiple Helpers: A sentence can have multiple helping verbs
  • Standalone: Main verbs can sometimes stand alone; helping verbs cannot

Types of Helping Verbs

Type Function Examples
Primary Auxiliaries Form tenses, questions, negatives be, have, do
Modal Auxiliaries Express ability, possibility, permission, obligation can, could, may, might, will, would, shall, should, must
Semi-modals Function like modals but have different forms need to, have to, ought to, used to

Examples in Sentences

  • She is reading a book. (is = helping, reading = main)
  • They have finished their homework. (have = helping, finished = main)
  • He can speak three languages. (can = helping, speak = main)
  • Do you understand the question? (Do = helping, understand = main)
  • She will have been studying for six hours. (will, have, been = helping, studying = main)

Identifying Main and Helping Verbs

To identify whether a verb is main or helping:

  1. Question Test: Can the verb form a question by itself? (Do you go? → "Do" is helping)
  2. Negation Test: Can "not" be inserted after it? (She does not know → "does" is helping)
  3. Meaning Test: Does it carry the main meaning or just assist? (In "is running," "running" has the action meaning)
  4. Tense Test: Does it change form to show tense? Helping verbs often show tense while main verbs show the action.

Action Verbs vs. Stative Verbs

Action verbs express physical or mental activities that someone or something does. Stative verbs describe states, conditions, feelings, thoughts, relationships, or measurements that exist rather than actions that are performed.

Key Characteristics:

  • Action Verbs: Show what the subject does (run, think, build)
  • Stative Verbs: Show what the subject is or has (know, own, prefer)
  • Continuous Forms: Action verbs can use -ing forms; stative verbs generally cannot
  • Volition: Action verbs involve volition (choice); stative verbs describe involuntary states

Categories of Stative Verbs

Category Function Examples
Thought & Opinion Mental states and beliefs know, believe, understand, think, suppose
Feelings & Emotions Emotional states like, love, hate, prefer, want, need
Senses & Perception Sensory experiences see, hear, smell, taste, feel, seem
Possession Ownership and belonging have, own, possess, belong, contain
Measurement Cost, weight, size cost, weigh, measure, equal
States of Being Existence and relationships be, exist, consist, involve, concern

Examples in Sentences

  • Action: She runs five miles every morning. (physical activity)
  • Stative: She knows the answer. (state of knowledge)
  • Action: I am thinking about the problem. (active mental process)
  • Stative: I think he's right. (state of opinion)
  • Action: The chef is tasting the soup. (deliberate action)
  • Stative: The soup tastes delicious. (sensory perception)

Verbs That Can Be Both Action and Stative

Some verbs have both action and stative meanings:

  • Have: She has a car. (stative - possession) vs. She is having dinner. (action - eating)
  • See: I see what you mean. (stative - understand) vs. I am seeing the doctor. (action - meeting)
  • Think: I think it's true. (stative - believe) vs. I am thinking about it. (action - considering)
  • Feel: I feel happy. (stative - emotion) vs. I am feeling the fabric. (action - touching)

Transitive vs. Intransitive Verbs

Transitive verbs require a direct object to complete their meaning. The action of the verb passes from the subject to the object. Intransitive verbs do not require a direct object; they express complete actions by themselves.

Key Characteristics:

  • Transitive Verbs: Must have a direct object (answers "what?" or "whom?")
  • Intransitive Verbs: Do not have a direct object (action doesn't pass to an object)
  • Both Types: Some verbs can be both transitive and intransitive
  • Passive Voice: Only transitive verbs can be used in passive voice

Identifying Transitive and Intransitive Verbs

Test Transitive Verb Intransitive Verb
Direct Object Test Can answer "what?" or "whom?" after the verb Cannot answer "what?" or "whom?" after the verb
Passive Voice Test Can be converted to passive voice Cannot be converted to passive voice
Sentence Completion Sentence feels incomplete without object Sentence is complete without object

Examples in Sentences

  • Transitive: She bought a new car. (What did she buy? A car - direct object)
  • Intransitive: He arrived early. (Cannot ask "what arrived?" - no direct object)
  • Transitive: The teacher explained the lesson. (What was explained? The lesson)
  • Intransitive: The sun rises in the east. (No direct object)
  • Both: She sings beautifully. (intransitive) vs. She sings songs. (transitive)

Verbs That Are Usually Transitive

These verbs typically require a direct object:

  • bring, buy, give, make, send, show, tell
  • build, create, design, develop
  • answer, ask, explain, teach
  • enjoy, like, love, hate, prefer

Verbs That Are Usually Intransitive

These verbs typically do not take a direct object:

  • arrive, come, go, leave, travel
  • sleep, rest, wake, dream
  • happen, occur, exist, disappear
  • laugh, cry, smile, sigh
  • rise, fall, appear, vanish

Phrasal Verbs

Phrasal verbs consist of a verb plus one or more particles (prepositions or adverbs) that together create a meaning different from the original verb. They are extremely common in English, especially in informal speech and writing.

Key Characteristics:

  • Verb + Particle: Combination creates new meaning
  • Idiomatic: Meaning often cannot be deduced from individual words
  • Separable/Inseparable: Some can be separated by object, others cannot
  • Transitive/Intransitive: Can be both depending on the phrasal verb

Types of Phrasal Verbs

Type Structure Example Notes
Intransitive verb + particle (no object) The car broke down. Cannot take an object
Transitive Separable verb + particle + object OR verb + object + particle Turn on the light. / Turn the light on. Object can come between verb and particle
Transitive Inseparable verb + particle + object She looks after her sister. Object must follow particle
Three-word verb + particle 1 + particle 2 I look forward to meeting you. Always inseparable

Common Phrasal Verbs by Meaning Category

1. Relationships & Social

  • get along with - have a good relationship with
  • break up with - end a romantic relationship
  • look up to - admire or respect
  • put up with - tolerate

2. Work & Study

  • take up - begin a hobby or activity
  • give up - quit or stop trying
  • catch up on - do work you should have done earlier
  • hand in - submit work

3. Communication

  • point out - indicate or mention
  • bring up - mention or introduce a topic
  • call off - cancel
  • figure out - understand or solve

4. Movement & Travel

  • set off - begin a journey
  • drop off - leave someone/something somewhere
  • pick up - collect someone/something
  • get away - go on vacation

Tips for Learning Phrasal Verbs

  1. Learn in Context: Memorize example sentences, not just definitions
  2. Group by Particle: Study phrasal verbs with the same particle together
  3. Note Separability: Pay attention to whether objects can come between verb and particle
  4. Practice Regularly: Use phrasal verbs in your own speaking and writing
  5. Watch for Formality: Many phrasal verbs are informal; know when to use more formal equivalents

Finite vs. Non-Finite Verbs

Finite verbs show tense and agree with their subjects. Non-finite verbs do not show tense or subject agreement and cannot be the main verb in a sentence. Every complete sentence must contain at least one finite verb.

Key Characteristics:

  • Finite Verbs: Show tense, person, and number; agree with subject
  • Non-Finite Verbs: Do not show tense, person, or number; don't change with subject
  • Three Forms: Non-finite verbs have three forms: infinitives, gerunds, and participles
  • Sentence Role: Finite verbs can be main verbs; non-finite verbs cannot stand alone

Comparison Table

Aspect Finite Verbs Non-Finite Verbs
Tense Show tense (present, past, future) Do not show tense
Subject Agreement Agree with subject (I am, you are, he is) Do not change according to subject
Function Can be main verb Cannot be main verb; need finite verb
Examples She writes. They wrote. He will write. To write, writing, written
Sentence Essential for complete sentence Optional; enhance meaning

Examples of Non-Finite Verb Forms

1. Infinitives (to + base verb)

  • As noun: To err is human.
  • As adjective: She has homework to complete.
  • As adverb: He studies to learn.
  • Bare infinitive: She can swim well.

2. Gerunds (verb + -ing as noun)

  • As subject: Swimming is good exercise.
  • As object: She enjoys reading.
  • After preposition: He's good at solving problems.

3. Participles (verb forms as adjectives)

  • Present participle: The barking dog woke everyone.
  • Past participle: The broken window needs repair.
  • Perfect participle: Having finished her work, she left.

Identifying Finite vs. Non-Finite Verbs

Ask these questions:

  1. Tense Test: Does it show when the action happens? (If yes → finite)
  2. Agreement Test: Does it change when the subject changes? (If yes → finite)
  3. Standalone Test: Can it be the only verb in a complete sentence? (If yes → finite)
  4. Form Test: Is it an infinitive (to + verb), gerund (-ing as noun), or participle (-ing/-ed as adjective)? (If yes → non-finite)

Important Note:

The same -ing form can be finite or non-finite depending on usage:

  • Finite: She is studying. (part of present continuous tense)
  • Non-finite: She enjoys studying. (gerund as object)
  • Non-finite: The studying student worked hard. (present participle as adjective)

Regular vs. Irregular Verbs

Regular verbs form their past tense and past participle by adding -ed to the base form. Irregular verbs have unique forms for past tense and past participle that must be memorized individually.

Key Characteristics:

  • Regular Verbs: Follow predictable pattern: base + -ed for past/past participle
  • Irregular Verbs: Have unique forms; no consistent pattern
  • Number: About 200 irregular verbs in common use
  • Frequency: Many common verbs are irregular (be, have, do, go, etc.)

Regular Verb Conjugation Patterns

Rule Base Form Past Tense Past Participle Example Sentence
Add -ed walk walked walked I walk → I walked → I have walked
Add -d (ends in e) live lived lived She lives → She lived → She has lived
Double consonant + -ed stop stopped stopped It stops → It stopped → It has stopped
Change y to i + -ed study studied studied He studies → He studied → He has studied

Common Irregular Verb Patterns

1. No Change (A-A-A Pattern)

  • cut - cut - cut
  • put - put - put
  • set - set - set
  • shut - shut - shut

2. Same Past and Past Participle (A-B-B Pattern)

  • bring - brought - brought
  • buy - bought - bought
  • catch - caught - caught
  • teach - taught - taught
  • think - thought - thought

3. Different All Three Forms (A-B-C Pattern)

  • begin - began - begun
  • drink - drank - drunk
  • ring - rang - rung
  • sing - sang - sung
  • swim - swam - swum

4. Vowel Change (i-a-u Pattern)

  • begin - began - begun
  • drink - drank - drunk
  • ring - rang - rung
  • sing - sang - sung
  • swim - swam - swum

5. -ought/-aught Ending

  • bring - brought - brought
  • buy - bought - bought
  • catch - caught - caught
  • teach - taught - taught
  • think - thought - thought

Most Common Irregular Verbs

These are the irregular verbs you'll encounter most frequently:

Base Form Past Tense Past Participle
be was/were been
have had had
do did done
go went gone
see saw seen
come came come
take took taken
make made made
know knew known
get got gotten/got

Tips for Learning Irregular Verbs

  1. Group by Pattern: Learn verbs with similar patterns together
  2. Use in Sentences: Practice with example sentences, not just memorizing forms
  3. Focus on Common Ones: Master the most frequently used irregular verbs first
  4. Create Flashcards: Regular practice with flashcards helps with memorization
  5. Listen and Repeat: Hearing correct usage helps internalize the patterns

Verb Tenses

Verb tenses indicate when an action or state occurs. English has 12 main tenses that combine time (past, present, future) with aspect (simple, continuous, perfect, perfect continuous).

Key Concepts:

  • Time: When the action occurs (past, present, future)
  • Aspect: How the action relates to time (simple, continuous, perfect, perfect continuous)
  • Formation: Tenses are formed using helping verbs + main verb forms
  • Usage: Each tense has specific uses and time expressions

The 12 English Tenses

Tense Structure Example Common Uses
Present Simple base form / base + s She writes letters. Habits, facts, general truths
Present Continuous am/is/are + present participle She is writing a letter. Actions happening now, temporary situations
Present Perfect have/has + past participle She has written letters. Past actions with present relevance, experiences
Present Perfect Continuous have/has been + present participle She has been writing letters. Actions started in past, continuing to present
Past Simple past form She wrote letters. Completed actions in definite past
Past Continuous was/were + present participle She was writing a letter. Actions in progress at specific past time
Past Perfect had + past participle She had written letters. Actions completed before another past action
Past Perfect Continuous had been + present participle She had been writing letters. Actions ongoing before another past action
Future Simple will + base form She will write letters. Predictions, spontaneous decisions
Future Continuous will be + present participle She will be writing letters. Actions in progress at specific future time
Future Perfect will have + past participle She will have written letters. Actions completed before specific future time
Future Perfect Continuous will have been + present participle She will have been writing letters. Actions ongoing up to specific future time

Time Expressions for Each Tense

Present Tenses

  • Simple: always, usually, often, sometimes, never, every day
  • Continuous: now, at the moment, currently, right now
  • Perfect: already, yet, just, ever, never, since, for
  • Perfect Continuous: for, since, all day, how long

Past Tenses

  • Simple: yesterday, last week, in 1999, ago
  • Continuous: while, when, as, at 5 PM yesterday
  • Perfect: by the time, before, after, already
  • Perfect Continuous: for, since, all day, how long

Future Tenses

  • Simple: tomorrow, next week, in 2024, soon
  • Continuous: at this time tomorrow, from 3 to 5
  • Perfect: by, by the time, before
  • Perfect Continuous: by, for, when

Common Tense Usage Errors

  1. Present Simple vs. Present Continuous: Don't use present continuous for permanent states
  2. Past Simple vs. Present Perfect: Use past simple for definite past time; present perfect for indefinite
  3. Will vs. Going to: "Will" for predictions/decisions; "going to" for plans
  4. Past Perfect Overuse: Only use when necessary to show sequence of past events
  5. Continuous with Stative Verbs: Avoid continuous forms with stative verbs (know, believe, etc.)

Voice and Mood

Voice indicates whether the subject performs or receives the action. Mood expresses the speaker's attitude toward the action. English has two voices (active and passive) and three moods (indicative, imperative, subjunctive).

Key Concepts:

  • Active Voice: Subject performs the action
  • Passive Voice: Subject receives the action
  • Indicative Mood: States facts or asks questions
  • Imperative Mood: Gives commands or makes requests
  • Subjunctive Mood: Expresses wishes, suggestions, or hypotheticals

Active vs. Passive Voice

Aspect Active Voice Passive Voice
Focus On the doer of the action On the receiver of the action
Structure Subject + Verb + Object Subject + be + Past Participle + (by + agent)
Example The chef cooked the meal. The meal was cooked by the chef.
Verb Type All verbs Only transitive verbs
Use When Doer is important or known Receiver is important or doer is unknown/unimportant

Passive Voice in Different Tenses

  • Present Simple: The letter is written by her.
  • Present Continuous: The letter is being written by her.
  • Present Perfect: The letter has been written by her.
  • Past Simple: The letter was written by her.
  • Past Continuous: The letter was being written by her.
  • Past Perfect: The letter had been written by her.
  • Future Simple: The letter will be written by her.
  • Future Perfect: The letter will have been written by her.

Verb Moods

1. Indicative Mood (Most Common)

Used for statements of fact and questions:

  • Statements: She studies every day. It is raining.
  • Questions: Do you understand? Where is she going?
  • Exclamations: What a beautiful day it is!

2. Imperative Mood

Used for commands, requests, and instructions:

  • Commands: Close the door. Be quiet.
  • Requests: Please help me. Pass the salt.
  • Instructions: First, mix the ingredients. Turn left at the corner.
  • Negative: Don't touch that. Never give up.
  • With "let's": Let's go. Let's not argue.

3. Subjunctive Mood

Used for wishes, suggestions, demands, and hypothetical situations:

Use Structure Example
Wishes wish + past subjunctive I wish I were taller.
Suggestions suggest/recommend + that + base form She suggested that he study more.
Demands demand/insist + that + base form I demand that she apologize.
Hypotheticals If + past subjunctive, would + base form If I were you, I would go.
Importance It's important + that + base form It's important that he be on time.

When to Use Passive Voice

  1. Unknown Agent: My car was stolen last night. (We don't know who stole it)
  2. Unimportant Agent: The road is being repaired. (Who is repairing it doesn't matter)
  3. Obvious Agent: She was elected president. (By voters - obvious)
  4. General Statements: English is spoken here.
  5. Formal Writing: The results were analyzed and conclusions were drawn.
  6. Tact or Politeness: Mistakes were made. (Rather than "You made mistakes")

Style Tip:

In most writing, active voice is preferred because it's more direct and concise. However, passive voice has specific uses where it's appropriate or necessary.
