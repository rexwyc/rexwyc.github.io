Conditional Grammar Guide

A complete reference for understanding the five main types of conditional sentences in English grammar. Master your structures, rules, and variations.

Zero Conditional

Used to describe general truths, scientific facts, habits, or rules. It refers to situations where one event always and automatically triggers another.

Structure: If + Present Simple, ... Present Simple

Examples:

Fact: If you heat water to 100°C, it boils.
Nature: When the sun goes down, it gets dark.

In this conditional type, the word "if" can almost always be swapped with "when" without changing the core meaning of the sentence.

First Conditional

Used to predict a real, highly possible, or likely future event and its logical consequence. It deals with specific future plans and outcomes.

Structure: If + Present Simple, ... Will + Verb

Examples:

Weather: If it rains tomorrow, we will cancel the picnic.
Action: If you don't hurry, we will miss the train.

Be careful: avoid using future tense markers like "will" inside the conditional 'if' clause itself (e.g., say "if it rains," not "if it will rain").

Second Conditional

Used to talk about imaginary, hypothetical, or completely unrealistic scenarios in the present or future, along with their expected results.

Structure: If + Past Simple, ... Would + Verb

Examples:

Dream: If I were a millionaire, I would buy an island.
Advice: I wouldn't buy that jacket if I were you.

Grammar Tip: The subjunctive form "were" is preferred over "was" for all subjects (I, he, she, it) in formal Second Conditional phrases.

Third Conditional

Used to speculate about completely unchangeable past events, creating a hypothetical past outcome. It often expresses regrets or relief.

Structure: If + Past Perfect, ... Would Have + Past Participle

Examples:

Regret: If you had asked me, I would have helped you.
Missed: She wouldn't have missed the flight if she had left on time.

This conditional is purely imaginary because the time window has passed, meaning the condition is completely impossible to fulfill now.

Mixed Conditional

Used when the timeframe of the condition and the timeframe of the consequence do not match (e.g., how a past action alters the present status).

Structure: If + Past Perfect, ... Would + Verb (and vice versa)

Examples:

Past to Pres: If I had won the lottery last year, I would be rich today.
Pres to Past: If he were a faster runner, he would have won yesterday.

Mixed conditionals bridge time barriers, commonly mapping a past occurrence directly onto a current present reality.
