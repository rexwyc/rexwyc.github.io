Zero Conditional (Type 0)

The zero conditional is used to talk about general truths, scientific facts, or habits. It describes situations where one action *always* causes another result under certain conditions.

Structure & Examples:

Structure: If + Present Simple, ... Present Simple
Fact: If you heat ice, it melts.
Habit: If I wake up late, I miss the bus.

In zero conditional sentences, the word "if" can usually be replaced by "when" without changing the core meaning of the statement.

First Conditional (Type 1)

The first conditional is used to express realistic, possible situations in the present or future, along with their highly probable or predictable outcomes.

Structure & Examples:

Structure: If + Present Simple, ... Will + Verb
Weather: If it rains tomorrow, we will stay home.
Action: If you study hard, you will pass the exam.

This structure focuses on real and possible future constraints. Modal verbs like "can," "may," or "might" can sometimes replace "will."

Second Conditional (Type 2)

The second conditional deals with imaginary, hypothetical, or highly improbable situations in the present or future, indicating outcomes that are unlikely to happen.

Structure & Examples:

Structure: If + Past Simple, ... Would + Verb
Dream: If I won the lottery, I would buy a mansion.
Advice: If I were you, I would take that job offer.

Note that "were" is traditionally used instead of "was" for all subjects (I, he, she, it) in formal grammar to indicate a subjective mood.

Third Conditional (Type 3)

The third conditional describes an unfulfilled situation in the past. It looks back at a past event that didn't happen and imagines an alternative past outcome.

Structure & Examples:

Structure: If + Past Perfect, ... Would Have + V3
Regret: If I had studied, I would have passed.
Missed: If they had arrived early, they wouldn't have missed it.

This structure is frequently used to express regrets, relief, or historical criticisms about actions that cannot be altered anymore.
