Active vs Passive Voice

Master the difference between active and passive voice: when to use each, how to transform sentences, and why voice matters in writing.

Active Voice

In active voice, the subject performs the action. It's direct, clear, and often more concise than passive voice. Most English sentences use active voice.

Key examples:

The chef cooked a delicious meal.
The team won the championship.
She writes beautiful poetry.

Active voice creates stronger, more engaging sentences. Use it when you want to emphasize who or what is doing the action.

Passive Voice Coming soon

In passive voice, the subject receives the action. It's formed with "be" + past participle. Use it when the doer is unknown, unimportant, or you want to emphasize the action itself.

Transformation examples:

Active: The chef cooked a meal → Passive: A meal was cooked (by the chef).
Active: The company launched a product → Passive: A product was launched.
Active: Someone stole my wallet → Passive: My wallet was stolen.

Passive voice is common in formal, scientific, or academic writing where the action matters more than the doer.
