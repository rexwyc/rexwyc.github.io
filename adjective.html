Descriptive Adjectives

Descriptive adjectives (also called qualitative adjectives) describe the quality, kind, or condition of a noun or pronoun. They add detail and color to language by answering questions like "What kind?" or "What is it like?"

Examples:

Color: red apple, blue sky, green grass
Size/Shape: huge building, tiny insect, round table
Quality/Opinion: beautiful painting, clever student, comfortable chair

Descriptive adjectives can be used attributively (before a noun) or predicatively (after a linking verb like "is" or "seems"). They are the most common type of adjective.

Order of Adjectives

When multiple adjectives modify the same noun, they must follow a specific order in English. The general sequence is: Opinion → Size → Age → Shape → Color → Origin → Material → Purpose.

Examples:

Correct: a beautiful large old round red French wooden dining table
Incorrect: a wooden French red old large beautiful dining table
Natural: a lovely small antique house

Using adjectives in the wrong order sounds unnatural to native speakers. Commas are used between coordinate adjectives (opinion and size), but not between cumulative adjectives (material and purpose).

Comparative & Superlative

Comparative adjectives compare differences between two objects (more, -er). Superlative adjectives compare three or more objects, indicating the highest degree (most, -est).

Examples:

Positive: tall, beautiful, good
Comparative: taller, more beautiful, better
Superlative: tallest, most beautiful, best

One-syllable adjectives generally take -er/-est. Three or more syllables use "more/most". Two-syllable adjectives can vary. Irregular forms include: good/better/best, bad/worse/worst, far/farther/farthest.

Proper Adjectives

Proper adjectives are derived from proper nouns and always begin with a capital letter. They often describe nationality, religion, language, or specific cultural concepts.

Examples:

Nationality/Origin: French cuisine, Japanese technology, Brazilian carnival
Religious/Cultural: Christian tradition, Shakespearean drama, Victorian era
Famous names: Einsteinian physics, Freudian slip, Orwellian society

Unlike common adjectives, proper adjectives maintain their capitalization. Many are formed by adding suffixes like -ian, -ese, -ish, -an, or -ic to proper nouns.
