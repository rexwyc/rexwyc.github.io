Personal Pronouns

What are Personal Pronouns?

Personal pronouns are words that replace specific nouns representing people, places, or things. They change form based on their function in a sentence (subject or object) and based on person (first, second, or third person).

Personal Pronoun Chart

Person Subject Pronoun Object Pronoun Example Sentences
First Person Singular I me I like coffee. Please give it to me.
Second Person Singular you you You are smart. I will call you later.
Third Person Singular (Male) he him He is tall. I saw him yesterday.
Third Person Singular (Female) she her She is a doctor. I talked to her.
Third Person Singular (Neutral) it it It is raining. I can't find it.
First Person Plural we us We are friends. They invited us.
Second Person Plural you you You all should come. I made this for you.
Third Person Plural they them They are students. I know them.

Usage Notes

  • Subject pronouns are used when the pronoun is the subject of the verb (performs the action).
  • Object pronouns are used when the pronoun is the object of the verb (receives the action).
  • The pronoun "they" can also be used as a singular pronoun for a person whose gender is unknown or non-binary.
  • Always match the pronoun with its antecedent (the noun it replaces) in number and gender.

Possessive Pronouns

What are Possessive Pronouns?

Possessive pronouns show ownership or possession without needing an apostrophe. They replace possessive noun phrases to avoid repetition.

Possessive Pronoun Chart

Person Possessive Adjective Possessive Pronoun Example Sentences
First Person Singular my mine This is my book. This book is mine.
Second Person Singular your yours Is this your pen? This pen is yours.
Third Person Singular (Male) his his That is his car. That car is his.
Third Person Singular (Female) her hers This is her bag. This bag is hers.
Third Person Singular (Neutral) its its (rarely used) The cat licked its paw.
First Person Plural our ours This is our house. This house is ours.
Second Person Plural your yours Are these your keys? These keys are yours.
Third Person Plural their theirs That is their dog. That dog is theirs.

Key Differences

  • Possessive adjectives always come before a noun (my book).
  • Possessive pronouns stand alone and replace the noun (This book is mine).
  • Unlike possessive nouns (John's car), possessive pronouns don't use apostrophes.
  • Possessive pronouns help avoid repetition: "Is this your coat?" "Yes, it's mine." (instead of "Yes, it's my coat.")

Reflexive & Intensive Pronouns

What are Reflexive and Intensive Pronouns?

Reflexive and intensive pronouns are formed by adding "-self" (singular) or "-selves" (plural) to certain personal pronouns. They look identical but serve different grammatical functions.

Reflexive & Intensive Pronoun Forms

Person Singular Plural Example Sentences
First Person myself ourselves I bought myself a gift. We enjoyed ourselves.
Second Person yourself yourselves Did you hurt yourself? You should be proud of yourselves.
Third Person (Male) himself themselves He taught himself to play guitar. They prepared themselves.
Third Person (Female) herself themselves She made herself breakfast. The team congratulated themselves.
Third Person (Neutral) itself themselves The cat cleaned itself. The doors opened by themselves.

Reflexive vs. Intensive Pronouns

  • Reflexive pronouns refer back to the subject and are necessary to the sentence meaning: "She cut herself."
  • Intensive pronouns emphasize a noun or pronoun and can be removed without changing the sentence's core meaning: "I myself saw the accident."
  • Reflexive pronouns are often used with verbs that describe actions people do to themselves (wash, dress, prepare, etc.).
  • Never use reflexive pronouns as subjects: Incorrect: "John and myself went shopping." Correct: "John and I went shopping."

Demonstrative Pronouns

What are Demonstrative Pronouns?

Demonstrative pronouns point to specific things or people. They indicate whether the referent is near or far in distance or time, and whether it's singular or plural.

Demonstrative Pronoun Chart

Pronoun Number Distance Example Sentences
this Singular Near (in space or time) This is my favorite book. (book is near me)
that Singular Far (in space or time) That was a great movie. (movie watched earlier)
these Plural Near (in space or time) These are delicious cookies. (cookies on the table)
those Plural Far (in space or time) Those were the best years of my life. (years in the past)

Demonstrative Pronouns vs. Demonstrative Adjectives

Function Example Explanation
Demonstrative Pronoun This is expensive. "This" stands alone as the subject of the sentence.
Demonstrative Adjective This car is expensive. "This" modifies the noun "car" and comes before it.
Demonstrative Pronoun I want those. "Those" stands alone as the object of the sentence.
Demonstrative Adjective I want those shoes. "Those" modifies the noun "shoes" and comes before it.

Usage Guidelines

  • Use this and these for things that are physically close or happening now.
  • Use that and those for things that are farther away or happened in the past.
  • Demonstrative pronouns can refer to both concrete objects and abstract ideas: "This is important."
  • When introducing people, use demonstrative pronouns: "This is my sister."
