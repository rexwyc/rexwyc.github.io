Finite vs. Non-Finite Verbs

A complete guide to understanding finite verbs, non-finite verbs, infinitives, gerunds, and participles in English grammar.

Finite Verbs

Finite verbs are verbs that show tense, person, and number. They agree with the subject and can stand alone as the main verb in a sentence.

Examples:

Present: She writes every day.
Past: They played soccer yesterday.
Future: I will call you tomorrow.

Finite verbs change form according to the subject (I am, you are, he is) and tense (I write, I wrote).

Non-Finite Verbs

Non-finite verbs do not show tense, person, or number. They cannot be the main verb in a sentence and must be used with a finite verb.

Examples:

Infinitive: She wants to dance.
Gerund: Swimming is good exercise.
Participle: The broken vase was expensive.

Non-finite verbs have three forms: infinitives, gerunds, and participles.

Infinitives

The infinitive is the base form of a verb, usually preceded by "to." It can function as a noun, adjective, or adverb in a sentence.

Examples:

Noun: To err is human.
Adjective: Water to drink is scarce.
Adverb: She studies to learn.

Infinitives can be used with or without "to" (bare infinitives), depending on the context.

View Full Section

Gerunds

Gerunds are verb forms ending in "-ing" that function as nouns. They can be subjects, objects, or complements in sentences.

Examples:

Subject: Running is my favorite exercise.
Object: I enjoy reading mystery novels.
Complement: Her hobby is painting.

Gerunds look like present participles but function as nouns rather than adjectives or parts of verb tenses.

View Full Section

Participles

Participles are verb forms that function as adjectives. There are two types: present participles (-ing) and past participles (-ed, -en, etc.).

Examples:

Present: The barking dog woke everyone.
Past: The broken window needs repair.
Perfect: Having finished her work, she left.

Participles can also be used to form verb tenses (continuous and perfect) when combined with auxiliary verbs.

Finite vs. Non-Finite

The key difference is that finite verbs show tense and agree with subjects, while non-finite verbs do not.

Examples:

Finite: She speaks French.
Non-Finite: She likes to speak French.
Finite: They are studying.
Non-Finite: They enjoy studying.

Every complete sentence must contain at least one finite verb, but non-finite verbs are optional.

Finite Verbs

Finite verbs are the backbone of English sentences. They are verbs that show tense, person, and number, and they agree with the subject. Every complete sentence must contain at least one finite verb.

Key Characteristics of Finite Verbs:

  • Show tense (present, past, future)
  • Agree with the subject in person and number
  • Can be the main verb in a sentence
  • Change form according to the subject
  • Can be used independently to form a complete sentence

Forms of Finite Verbs

Tense First Person (I/We) Second Person (You) Third Person (He/She/It/They)
Present Simple I write You write He/She/It writes
Past Simple I wrote You wrote He/She/It wrote
Future Simple I will write You will write He/She/It will write
Present Continuous I am writing You are writing He/She/It is writing

Examples in Sentences

  • She speaks three languages fluently. (Present tense, third person singular)
  • We studied for the exam last night. (Past tense, first person plural)
  • They will arrive tomorrow morning. (Future tense, third person plural)
  • I am reading an interesting book. (Present continuous, first person singular)
  • You have completed the assignment. (Present perfect, second person)

Identifying Finite Verbs

To identify a finite verb, ask these questions:

  1. Does it show tense? (Is it happening now, in the past, or in the future?)
  2. Does it agree with the subject? (I am, you are, he is)
  3. Can it stand alone as the main verb in a sentence?
  4. Does it change form when the subject or tense changes?

Important Note:

Auxiliary verbs (be, have, do, will, would, can, could, etc.) are also finite when they show tense and agreement with the subject. For example, in "She is studying," "is" is a finite auxiliary verb.

Non-Finite Verbs

Non-finite verbs are verb forms that do not show tense, person, or number. They cannot function as the main verb in a sentence and must be used alongside a finite verb. Non-finite verbs have three main forms: infinitives, gerunds, and participles.

Key Characteristics of Non-Finite Verbs:

  • Do not show tense (present, past, or future)
  • Do not change form according to the subject
  • Cannot be the main verb in a sentence
  • Must be used with a finite verb to form a complete sentence
  • Can function as nouns, adjectives, or adverbs

Types of Non-Finite Verbs

Type Form Function Example
Infinitive to + base verb
(or bare infinitive)		 Noun, adjective, adverb She wants to dance.
Gerund verb + -ing Noun Swimming is fun.
Present Participle verb + -ing Adjective, part of continuous tenses The crying baby needs attention.
Past Participle verb + -ed, -en, or irregular form Adjective, part of perfect tenses The broken vase was expensive.

Examples in Sentences

  • Infinitive: I want to learn French. (functions as noun - object of verb)
  • Gerund: Running is good for health. (functions as noun - subject)
  • Present Participle: The shining sun warmed the beach. (functions as adjective)
  • Past Participle: The written exam was difficult. (functions as adjective)
  • Perfect Participle: Having finished his work, he went home. (functions as adverb)

Identifying Non-Finite Verbs

To identify a non-finite verb, ask these questions:

  1. Does it show tense? (If no, it's likely non-finite)
  2. Does it change form when the subject changes? (Non-finite verbs don't)
  3. Can it stand alone as the main verb? (Non-finite verbs can't)
  4. Does it function as a noun, adjective, or adverb rather than as an action?

Important Note:

The same verb form can sometimes be finite or non-finite depending on its use in the sentence. For example, "running" in "She is running" (finite - part of continuous tense) vs. "Running is fun" (non-finite - gerund functioning as noun).

Infinitives

The infinitive is the base form of a verb, usually preceded by "to." It is one of the three types of non-finite verbs and can function as a noun, adjective, or adverb in a sentence. Infinitives without "to" are called bare infinitives.

Key Characteristics of Infinitives:

  • Base form of the verb, usually with "to"
  • Can function as noun, adjective, or adverb
  • Does not show tense, person, or number
  • Often used after certain verbs (want, need, decide, etc.)
  • Bare infinitives (without "to") are used after modal verbs and some other verbs

Types of Infinitives

Type Form Example Function
Full Infinitive to + base verb She wants to go. Most common form
Bare Infinitive base verb (without "to") She can go. After modals and some verbs
Perfect Infinitive to have + past participle She seems to have gone. Refers to past time
Continuous Infinitive to be + present participle She appears to be working. Refers to ongoing action
Passive Infinitive to be + past participle The work needs to be done. Passive voice

Functions of Infinitives

1. Infinitives as Nouns

  • Subject: To err is human.
  • Object: She wants to learn Spanish.
  • Subject Complement: Her goal is to graduate with honors.
  • Appositive: His dream, to travel the world, finally came true.

2. Infinitives as Adjectives

  • I need a book to read on the plane.
  • She was the first person to arrive.
  • That's the best way to learn a language.

3. Infinitives as Adverbs

  • Purpose: He studies hard to succeed.
  • Result: She was too tired to continue.
  • Reason: I'm happy to help you.

Verbs Followed by Infinitives

Some common verbs that are typically followed by infinitives:

  • Verbs of wanting/desiring: want, wish, hope, desire, long, yearn
  • Verbs of planning/deciding: plan, decide, intend, mean, choose
  • Verbs of attempting: try, attempt, endeavor, strive
  • Other common verbs: need, promise, agree, refuse, seem, appear, tend

When to Use Bare Infinitives (Without "To")

  1. After modal verbs: can, could, may, might, will, would, shall, should, must
    • She can speak three languages.
  2. After certain verbs: let, make, help (sometimes), see, hear, feel, watch, notice
    • They let him leave early.
  3. After "had better," "would rather," and "why not"
    • You had better study for the test.
  4. In imperative sentences
    • Close the door, please.

Important Note:

The word "to" is not part of the infinitive when it functions as a preposition. Compare: "I'm looking forward to seeing you" (preposition + gerund) vs. "I want to see you" (infinitive).

Gerunds

Gerunds are verb forms ending in "-ing" that function as nouns. They are one of the three types of non-finite verbs. Gerunds look identical to present participles but serve different grammatical functions.

Key Characteristics of Gerunds:

  • Formed by adding "-ing" to the base verb
  • Function as nouns (subjects, objects, complements)
  • Can be modified by adjectives and adverbs
  • Can have objects and complements
  • Can be used after prepositions

Functions of Gerunds

Function Example Explanation
Subject Swimming is good exercise. Gerund as the subject of the sentence
Direct Object She enjoys reading. Gerund as the object of the verb "enjoys"
Subject Complement His favorite activity is hiking. Gerund follows linking verb "is"
Object of Preposition He's excited about traveling. Gerund follows preposition "about"
Appositive Her passion, painting, brings her joy. Gerund renames or explains "passion"

Gerunds with Objects and Modifiers

Gerunds can be part of gerund phrases that include objects, complements, and modifiers:

  • With object: Eating vegetables is healthy. ("vegetables" is the object of "eating")
  • With adverb: Driving carefully is important. ("carefully" modifies "driving")
  • With adjective: Rapid reading requires practice. ("rapid" modifies "reading")
  • With possessive: I appreciate your helping me. ("your" modifies "helping")

Verbs Followed by Gerunds

Some common verbs that are typically followed by gerunds:

  • Verbs of liking/disliking: enjoy, like, love, hate, dislike, adore, detest
  • Verbs of continuing: keep, continue, go on, carry on
  • Verbs of admitting/denying: admit, deny, confess
  • Verbs of considering: consider, imagine, contemplate
  • Verbs of avoiding: avoid, evade, escape
  • Other common verbs: finish, practice, suggest, recommend, mind, miss

Gerunds After Prepositions

Gerunds are always used after prepositions (not infinitives):

  • She's good at solving problems.
  • He's interested in learning French.
  • They talked about moving to a new city.
  • I look forward to hearing from you.

Gerund vs. Present Participle

Although they look identical, gerunds and present participles have different functions:

Aspect Gerund Present Participle
Function Functions as a noun Functions as an adjective or part of verb tense
Example as noun Swimming is fun. (subject) N/A
Example as adjective N/A The swimming pool is large. (modifies "pool")
Example in verb tense N/A She is swimming now. (part of continuous tense)

Special Cases and Common Mistakes

  1. Verbs that can take both gerunds and infinitives with little difference: begin, start, continue, like, love, hate, prefer
    • She started to cry / She started crying.
  2. Verbs that change meaning with gerund vs. infinitive:
    • I stopped smoking. (quit the habit)
    • I stopped to smoke. (stopped in order to smoke)
    • She remembered posting the letter. (memory of past action)
    • She remembered to post the letter. (didn't forget to do it)
  3. Gerunds after certain expressions: it's no use, it's worth, there's no point in, have difficulty/trouble
    • It's no use crying over spilled milk.

Participles

Participles are verb forms that function as adjectives or are used to form verb tenses. There are two main types: present participles (ending in -ing) and past participles (ending in -ed, -en, or irregular forms). Participles are non-finite verbs that do not show tense on their own.

Key Characteristics of Participles:

  • Present participles end in "-ing"
  • Past participles usually end in "-ed," "-en," "-d," "-t," or are irregular
  • Function as adjectives to modify nouns
  • Used with auxiliary verbs to form verb tenses
  • Can introduce participle phrases

Types of Participles

Type Form Example as Adjective Example in Verb Tense
Present Participle verb + -ing The barking dog She is barking orders.
Past Participle (Regular) verb + -ed The closed door She has closed the door.
Past Participle (Irregular) various forms The written exam She has written the exam.
Perfect Participle having + past participle Having finished her work... N/A (used in participle phrases)

Participles as Adjectives

Both present and past participles can function as adjectives:

Present Participles as Adjectives

Describe what something is doing or what characteristic it has (active meaning):

  • The boring lecture put everyone to sleep. (The lecture bores people.)
  • We saw falling leaves everywhere. (The leaves are falling.)
  • It was a thrilling experience. (The experience thrills people.)

Past Participles as Adjectives

Describe how something is affected (passive meaning):

  • The bored students yawned. (The students are bored by something.)
  • We collected the fallen leaves. (The leaves have fallen.)
  • She was thrilled by the news. (She was thrilled by something.)

Remember:

Present participles as adjectives have an active meaning (boring = causes boredom). Past participles as adjectives have a passive meaning (bored = affected by boredom).

Participles in Verb Tenses

Participles are essential for forming various verb tenses:

Tense Structure Example
Present Continuous am/is/are + present participle She is studying.
Past Continuous was/were + present participle They were playing.
Present Perfect have/has + past participle I have finished.
Past Perfect had + past participle She had left before I arrived.
Future Perfect will have + past participle By tomorrow, I will have completed it.
All Passive Tenses be + past participle The book was written by her.

Participle Phrases

Participles can introduce phrases that function as adjectives. These participle phrases usually appear at the beginning or end of sentences:

Present Participle Phrases

  • Hearing the news, she jumped for joy.
  • The man talking on the phone is my boss.
  • Walking down the street, I saw an old friend.

Past Participle Phrases

  • Exhausted by the journey, he went straight to bed.
  • The book written by the famous author became a bestseller.
  • Built in the 18th century, the house has historical significance.

Perfect Participle Phrases

  • Having finished her work, she went home.
  • Having been told the news, she immediately called her family.

Dangling Participles

A common error is the "dangling participle," where the participle phrase doesn't logically modify the noun it's supposed to:

Incorrect (Dangling):

Running down the street, the dog chased the cat. (This implies the dog was running down the street, but grammatically "running" modifies "dog," suggesting the dog was doing the running.)

Correct:

Running down the street, the cat was chased by the dog. OR As the dog ran down the street, it chased the cat.

Irregular Past Participles

Many common verbs have irregular past participles. Here are some examples:

  • be been
  • begin begun
  • break broken
  • choose chosen
  • do done
  • drive driven
  • eat eaten
  • fly flown
  • go gone
  • see seen
  • take taken
  • write written

Finite vs. Non-Finite Verbs: Comparison

Understanding the difference between finite and non-finite verbs is crucial for constructing grammatically correct sentences. This comparison highlights their key distinctions.

Core Difference:

Finite verbs show tense and agree with subjects; non-finite verbs do not. Every complete sentence must contain at least one finite verb.

Side-by-Side Comparison

Aspect Finite Verbs Non-Finite Verbs
Tense Show tense (present, past, future) Do not show tense
Subject Agreement Agree with subject in person and number Do not change according to subject
Function in Sentence Can be main verb Cannot be main verb; need finite verb
Forms All conjugated forms (am, is, are, was, were, etc.) Infinitives, gerunds, participles
Grammatical Role Express action or state Function as nouns, adjectives, adverbs
Examples She writes. They wrote. He will write. To write, writing, written
Sentence Requirement Essential for complete sentence Optional; enhance sentence meaning

Examples in Context

Contrasting Examples:

  • Finite: She teaches English. (Shows present tense, agrees with "she")
  • Non-finite: She likes to teach English. ("to teach" is infinitive, doesn't show tense)
  • Finite: They are studying for exams. (Shows present continuous tense)
  • Non-finite: They enjoy studying for exams. ("studying" is gerund, functions as noun)
  • Finite: The dog barked loudly. (Shows past tense)
  • Non-finite: The barking dog woke everyone. ("barking" is participle, functions as adjective)

How They Work Together

Finite and non-finite verbs often work together in sentences:

Sentence Finite Verb Non-Finite Verb Type
She wants to learn French. wants to learn Infinitive
Running is good exercise. is Running Gerund
The broken window needs repair. needs broken Past Participle
He is writing a letter. is writing Present Participle (in continuous tense)
Having finished his work, he left. left Having finished Perfect Participle

Quick Identification Guide

Is it finite? Ask these questions:

  1. Does it show when the action happens (present, past, future)?
  2. Does it change when the subject changes (I am, you are, he is)?
  3. Can it be the only verb in a sentence that makes sense?
  4. If yes to any of these, it's finite.

Is it non-finite? Look for these forms:

  1. Infinitives: "to" + base verb (or bare infinitive without "to")
  2. Gerunds: verb + -ing functioning as a noun
  3. Participles:
    • Present participles: verb + -ing functioning as adjective
    • Past participles: verb + -ed, -en, or irregular form functioning as adjective

Key Takeaway:

If you can change the tense of a verb in a sentence, it's finite. If the verb form stays the same regardless of tense or subject changes, it's non-finite.

Example: "She likes to swim." "She liked to swim." ("likes" changed to "liked" = finite; "to swim" stayed the same = non-finite)

Common Mistakes to Avoid

  1. Confusing gerunds and present participles: Remember that gerunds function as nouns, while present participles function as adjectives or parts of verb tenses.
  2. Thinking "-ing" forms are always non-finite: When "-ing" forms are part of continuous tenses (is running, was eating), they are finite because they show tense through the auxiliary verb.
  3. Overlooking finite auxiliary verbs: In "She is studying," both "is" (finite) and "studying" (non-finite) are verbs, but only "is" is finite.
  4. Forgetting that infinitives can be bare: "She can swim" contains the bare infinitive "swim" (non-finite) after the modal verb "can" (finite).
